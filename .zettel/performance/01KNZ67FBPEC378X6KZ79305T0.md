*Source: David Daly, William Brown, Henrik Ingo, Jim O'Leary, David Bradford — "The Use of Change Point Detection to Identify Software Performance Regressions in a Continuous Integration System" — Proceedings of the 2020 ACM/SPEC International Conference on Performance Engineering (ICPE '20), April 2020. DOI 10.1145/3358960.3375791.*

This is the canonical industrial reference for how to actually do automatic performance regression detection in a CI system at scale. MongoDB ran into the problem, built a system, shipped it in production on their Evergreen CI, and wrote it up. Everyone doing perf gating should read this paper before reinventing the wheel.

## The problem MongoDB ran into

MongoDB runs a large performance test suite as part of their CI. The initial approach was the one every team tries first: **compare commit N's benchmark result to commit N-1 with a fixed threshold (e.g. `alert if > 10% slower`).** This approach failed in practice for the reasons every team discovers:

1. **Noise swamps the signal.** Run-to-run variance on many benchmarks routinely exceeds the regression threshold. Pairwise comparison across adjacent commits produces a blizzard of false positives — a sheriff on rotation cannot distinguish real regressions from noise, and eventually ignores the channel (alert fatigue).
2. **Slow drifts are invisible.** A genuine regression that accumulates as three commits at 4% each is invisible to a 10% pairwise threshold, but a 12% cliff is very visible over the 3-commit window if you look at it as a time series.
3. **Noise is non-stationary.** Hardware replacements, OS upgrades, and test harness changes cause shifts in the baseline that are not regressions but would trip any fixed threshold.

The key realisation is that **the natural object of analysis is not a pair of commits but a time series of measurements**, and the natural question is not "did this commit regress?" but "at what point in history did the distribution shift?"

## The approach: E-divisive means change-point detection

The paper adopts the **E-divisive means** algorithm (Matteson and James, 2014), a non-parametric change-point detection method based on **energy statistics**. The core idea:

- Given a time series, find the point `τ` that maximally splits the series into two segments such that the segments have different distributions.
- The statistic used is based on the expected Euclidean distance between samples drawn from one segment and samples drawn from the other, minus within-segment distances — this is a U-statistic estimator of **energy distance**, which is zero iff the two distributions are identical.
- Statistical significance of a candidate change-point is assessed by a **permutation test**: shuffle labels many times and see how often the observed statistic is exceeded under the null hypothesis of no change-point.
- The "divisive" part: recurse on each of the two sub-segments to find additional change-points.

**Why this beats a t-test on windows:**
- E-divisive is non-parametric — it makes no assumption that the noise is Gaussian, which is important because benchmark distributions are commonly multimodal, heavy-tailed, or right-skewed (coordinated omission artifacts, GC pauses, thermal throttling, co-tenant noise).
- It handles multiple change-points in a single pass, so a drift composed of several small regressions is caught as a single shift.
- It naturally reports *where* in the history the shift occurred, which is exactly what the human sheriff needs to blame a commit.

## Deployment at MongoDB

MongoDB integrated E-divisive into their Evergreen CI such that after each new benchmark run, the change-point detector re-processes the time series for that benchmark and surfaces new candidate change-points on a sheriff dashboard. A human performance sheriff then triages: confirm the regression, assign blame to a commit, file a ticket. The paper emphasises that **the algorithm does not make the final decision** — it produces a ranked list, and humans confirm actionability. This is deliberate: false positives are still a concern, and triage on a sheriff rotation benefits from a human-in-the-loop.

Design decisions worth highlighting:
- **Per-benchmark detection**, not cross-benchmark. Each time series is treated independently; aggregating across benchmarks loses information about which test regressed.
- **Permutation-based significance**, not t-test. The trade-off is that permutation tests are stochastic — two runs of the same analysis can return slightly different p-values. Hunter (Fleming et al. 2023, see `01K...`) later replaces permutation with a deterministic t-test-based significance test for exactly this reason.
- **Triage as an engineering discipline.** The paper argues that the CPD system's value is unlocked only when there is a rotation of humans committed to triaging every alert. Without the human loop, the dashboard becomes wallpaper.

## Why this is the canonical reference

Before Daly et al. 2020, the folklore in CI perf testing was a grab-bag of threshold-based heuristics and ad-hoc dashboards. This paper is the first industrial publication to:
1. Clearly articulate why fixed-threshold pairwise comparison fails.
2. Adopt a proper statistical change-point detection method from the statistics literature.
3. Report the operational experience of running it in production on a large CI system.
4. Open-source the building block: MongoDB's `signal-processing-algorithms` Python library (see dedicated note) implements E-divisive means and is available on PyPI.

## Adversarial commentary

The paper is industrial-strength but inherits the usual limitations of change-point detection:

- **Change-point detectors are retrospective.** By design, they need enough post-change data to establish the new regime — typically a few commits. This means regressions are detected hours-to-days after they land, not at PR time. For PR-level gating you still need something else (a short-run smoke benchmark with a looser threshold, or a statistical comparison against a rolling baseline).
- **Permutation-test non-determinism** means the same history can yield different alerts on different runs, which is confusing for sheriffs. Hunter replaces this with deterministic t-tests; the trade-off is that t-tests re-introduce a normality assumption.
- **The E-divisive statistic assumes independence between observations** within a segment. Benchmark time series frequently violate this — e.g., a flaky test that deteriorates slowly because of a resource leak on the CI runner looks autocorrelated, and the permutation significance test is optimistic in the presence of autocorrelation. MongoDB mitigates this operationally via hardware refresh, but the assumption is a real limitation.
- **No causal model.** CPD says "a shift happened somewhere in this commit range"; it does not tell you which commit caused it. MongoDB addresses this by having short commit ranges (Evergreen runs benchmarks often), but sparser CIs still need a bisection step (cf. Chrome Pinpoint).
- **Benchmark curation is out of scope.** The paper assumes the benchmark suite is already good. In practice, a significant fraction of noisy benchmarks are noisy *because* of design bugs (e.g., timing-dependent IO) and the CPD system surfaces the noise rather than fixing it. Someone still has to invest in benchmark hygiene.

## Connections

- Precedes Hunter (Fleming et al. ICPE 2023) which builds on the same E-divisive core but replaces permutation tests with deterministic t-tests.
- Related to the broader change-point literature: PELT (Killick et al. 2012), Bayesian change-point (Adams & MacKay 2007), binary segmentation.
- The MongoDB `signal-processing-algorithms` library is the open-source building block.
- Applies the non-parametric rationale that Mytkowicz et al. 2009 and Chen & Revels 2016 argue is necessary for performance data.

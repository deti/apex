# Capstone Synthesis — Performance Testing &amp; Automated Performance Test Generation

This note is the opinionated research artifact for the `.zettel/performance` vault. It compresses ~255 research notes / ~1,684 edges into an argument. It is deliberately opinionated: where a neutral framing would obscure the real trade-off, I take a side. The claim-level citations point at vault notes (ULIDs) where the evidence and sources sit.

The reader's goal is one of two things:

1. **Understand the state of performance testing well enough to make product-strategy decisions about a test-generation tool.**
2. **Pick specific research gaps to attack.**

Both are served by the same document; the gap-ranking section is the second-reader's landing pad.

---

## 0. Thesis

Performance testing as a discipline has three hard problems. Only one of them is being worked on by the vendor ecosystem.

- **The measurement problem** — "is this number real?" — has been solved in principle for two decades and is *still* mis-implemented in most commercial and open-source tools. The fixes (HdrHistogram, open-loop workloads, non-parametric regression statistics) are not secrets, they are ignored.
- **The workload problem** — "does this test represent the traffic we will actually see?" — is the defining open problem of the field. Every claim about test realism ultimately reduces to a claim about the workload model, and workload models are brittle, artisanal, and drift silently out of correspondence with production.
- **The oracle problem** — "did the test pass?" — is locally solved (SLOs as oracles) and globally open (metamorphic relations, differential comparison, change-point detection). In the LLM-test-generation hype cycle it is the most reliably ignored question.

The tooling market (k6, Gatling, JMeter, BlazeMeter, NeoLoad; see `01KNZ5F8VABE5TGW976NMQA1VP` matrix) sells solutions to the *scripting* problem — how do I express a load plan? — which is the least important of the three. The research frontier (DeepREST, Fuzz4All, PerfGen, NeoLoad MCP) is bolting LLMs onto the scripting problem and inheriting all three unsolved problems underneath.

**The high-leverage move for a new tool is not a better scripter or a better recorder. It is a workload-derivation pipeline bonded to a statistical oracle.**

This synthesis defends that claim.

## 1. Taxonomy of performance testing

### The types and what question each answers

Performance testing is not one activity, it is a family of activities that differ in the *question* being asked. The canonical decomposition (see `01KNZ4VB6JVSPDK724EZFPA36H` Meier et al. 2007; `01KNZ4VB6JJ51X8KRGKY6VH2W8` ISO/IEC 25010) is:

| Type | Question | SDLC placement | Anti-pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Load** (`01KNZ4VB6JY38THW04Z3MMGBZ3`) | "Does it meet SLOs at expected load?" | Pre-release, nightly | Running at nominal load and calling it "tested for production" |
| **Stress** (`01KNZ4VB6JTC1Z9CGYN4Q1CCA6`) | "Where does it break, and *how*?" | Pre-release, capacity planning | Treating graceful-degradation stress as a load pass/fail gate |
| **Spike** (`01KNZ4VB6JCKKSRJ6FE6ST9183`) | "How fast does it recover from a transient?" | Pre-release | Zero ramp-up hides autoscaling behaviour the test was supposed to find |
| **Soak / endurance** (`01KNZ4VB6J29PS11RZNH5K0E47`) | "Do resources leak or drift over long runs?" | Pre-release, weekly | Running for 1 hour because CI has time budget; the bugs the test finds take 6 |
| **Scalability** (`01KNZ4VB6JY3QDARVD4N06HR6X`) | "What is the shape of the throughput-vs-load curve?" | Capacity planning | Reporting a linear scale factor without fitting the Universal Scalability Law |
| **Volume** (`01KNZ4VB6JJ702SZ7R31SMAJG2`) | "How does it behave at large data sizes?" | Pre-release | Testing with synthetic uniform data when production is power-law |
| **Capacity** | "How much headroom is there?" | Quarterly, ops | Conflating with load testing; different oracle (breakpoint, not SLO) |
| **Configuration / isolation** (`01KNZ4VB6JRSN6YXB4KC63Y90K`) | "Do tuning knobs matter?" / "Does noise come from neighbours?" | Architecture, tuning | Running without resource isolation and attributing noise to code |

**Opinionated cut:** the first three types (load, stress, spike) are what most teams mean when they say "performance test," and they are the *easiest* to automate the script-generation half of. Soak and scalability are where the hard science actually lives, and they are the ones least covered by generated-test tools. A product that claimed to generate soak tests from traces would be genuinely novel; a product that generates a load-test for an OpenAPI schema (see `01KNZ4TTX5V1TESBMRM80J38XA`) is a thin wrapper over `openapi-generator`.

### Anti-patterns that recur across types

1. **Treating "no error" as pass.** A load test that saturates a queue at 99% depth but never errors is *failing* — queueing theory tells you latency is diverging. (`01KNZ4VB6J6R3V3GVBWSAKW8JC` operational laws, `01KNZ4VB6J4TER1QCE9CKABBED` Little's Law.)
2. **Closed-loop generators at benchmark scale.** The most subtle bug in the field; covered below in §3.
3. **Running tests in CI with the same generator threading model as production load, on a laptop-class runner.** Coordinated omission (`01KNZ4VB6JB4Q5H3NPS72MZZ2A`) eats tail latency silently, and the numbers you report are meaningless.
4. **"Percentile-over-percentile"** reporting — averaging p99s across runs, or computing a p99 of per-second p99s, both of which are statistical nonsense. (`01KNZ4VB6JCD7A2BMXFN1AWGP4` percentile composition math.)

## 2. The performance testing workflow

The literature's canonical workflow is a pipeline:

```
goals → workload model → environment → test design → execution → analysis → gating
```

The useful question is: **which stages are human-judgment-limited, and which are automatable?**

| Stage | Human-limited because... | Automation target? |
|---|---|---|
| Goal definition (`01KNZ4VB6JK3TC0S5YZWHNNDEV`) | Requires talking to the business and turning "it should be fast" into a distribution objective | Partial: LLMs can draft SLOs from PRDs; humans must approve |
| SLO-as-oracle (`01KNZ4VB6JQZHJVB2EQK6HVXE0`) | Requires understanding user tolerance curves | Yes — codifiable once defined (OpenSLO, Nobl9) |
| Workload modelling (`01KNZ4VB6JHJSARKD8E9XVGVRC`) | Requires production telemetry AND domain judgment about future traffic | **Highest-leverage automation target** (see §3, §6) |
| Environment parity (`01KNZ4VB6J22PTMXAYQ3V2WYAZ`) | Requires infra budget; no tool can grant you a prod clone | No — organisational problem |
| Test design | Requires picking the *right* question from the taxonomy | Partial: library of test templates per question |
| Execution | Infra-and-budget-bound | Yes — SaaS (BlazeMeter, Grafana Cloud k6) already does this |
| Analysis | Statistical expertise, distributional reasoning | Partially automated; done badly by most tools |
| Gating (`01KNZ4VB6J5ZW3JERZNDNGP7GD`) | Requires a concept of "practically significant" not just "statistically significant" | Yes — but the market uses the wrong statistics (see §7) |

**Opinionated cut:** automation opportunity stacks in the workload-modelling and analysis stages. The industry has instead automated execution (k6 Cloud) and scripting (Recorder tools, `01KNZ6GW3GYN9ZDT3A9JTVJFEW`) because those are the stages where customers can *see* the time savings. The invisible waste is in the other stages.

### Brendan Gregg's methodologies as the honest alternative

Gregg's body of work (`01KNZ4VB6J08D14Y8P3RWVAABA`) flips the workflow: instead of "build a workload, run it, read numbers," his methodologies (USE, `01KNZ4VB6JHP7W47HM7QREWW53`; ad-hoc-checklist; tools method; thread-state analysis) start from the running system and ask "where is the bottleneck?" This is the workflow of an on-call engineer, not a release gate. The two workflows are complementary: Gregg's tells you *what is happening*; the gating workflow tells you *whether the change you made broke anything*. Most teams do one and not the other, and it shows.

## 3. Workload modelling — the defining open problem

### Realistic workloads are harder than they look

Three canonical ways to build one, in decreasing order of fidelity and increasing order of tractability:

1. **Replay production traces** (`01KNZ4VB6J56B59YB7SZDKTAKD`). Highest fidelity, but: PII concerns, state issues (token expiry, idempotency keys, resource ID collision), sample bias, format drift. Tools: GoReplay (`01KNZ5F4WQ8VSNJBFYJFDSX7NT`), tcpreplay, Envoy tap (`01KNZ5F52X5746A9ASY0W6DKDS`), AWS VPC mirroring. The **replay divergence problem** (`01KNZ5F59R6M5ATD9X2YW87XAC`) is the single biggest failure mode.
2. **Synthetic distributions with fitted parameters.** Medium fidelity, higher portability. Fit arrival process (`01KNZ4VB6JNFK2XFWP9N1HEJ5M` Poisson), endpoint mix, payload-size distribution, think time (`01KNZ4VB6J3AB4QA4YZVDPMFWY`) from telemetry. Tools: almost none end-to-end; most load testers give you the *generator* but none give you the *fitting* step.
3. **User journey analysis.** The CBMG model (`01KNZ5ZPPXW26VRNZ9BHKB0AYV`, Menascé &amp; Almeida) — customer behaviour as a Markov chain over request types — is the oldest and best-specified framework for workload modelling in the literature. It is taught in one textbook (`01KNZ6QBH0YZYKPZNYDCZD5P2B`) and implemented in zero mainstream load tools. Session mining (`01KNZ5ZPVXKFXJPF3MNWTYXMZ1`) is the empirical input; worst-case traversals (`01KNZ5ZPSEK679QQYMHXF16WFF`) are an interesting application.

### Open vs closed — the mistake nobody told you you were making

Schroeder, Wierman, Harchol-Balter NSDI 2006 (`01KNZ4VB6JX0CQ5RFAZDJTQMCS`) is the must-read of the field, and the single most-ignored result. The paper's bottom line:

- **Closed-loop** generators (N concurrent users, each waits for response before issuing next) *cap their own throughput*. Under saturation, they hide the queue — latency stays bounded because the generator stops offering load.
- **Open-loop** generators (arrivals follow an external process, e.g. Poisson at rate λ) *do not cap*. Under saturation, latency explodes visibly, which is the correct behaviour for a test that exists to find the saturation point.
- **Thread-per-user tools default closed-loop.** JMeter, Locust-by-default, wrk-with-threads-equals-clients, Gatling-without-`constantUsersPerSec`, k6-with-`vus`-only. All of these give misleading results near saturation.
- **Correct usage is explicit.** Gatling's `constantUsersPerSec` / `rampUsersPerSec` (`01KNZ56MRW2B1XSH2X5K5AEJ33`), k6's `constant-arrival-rate` scenario (`01KNZ56MPVSZD05KM395ZKAM5J`), Vegeta's `-rate` flag (`01KNZ56MSFAM3EFBB1B48JKEWT`), wrk2's fixed-throughput mode (`01KNZ56MSNRKGMDGT9B745HVEB`), Fortio's `-qps` flag (`01KNZ5F8TVW837C1YJKKXFH504`). These are the honest tools.

**Adversarial test:** pick a team's existing load-test config and ask "is this open or closed?" The answer is almost always "we didn't know we had to choose," which means the config is closed-loop by default and every result they've ever reported is optimistic about saturation behaviour. This is not a minor caveat; it is a structural bug that invalidates most published internal benchmarks.

### The heavy-tail reality and think-time zeroing

Production traffic is self-similar and heavy-tailed (`01KNZ4VB6JSR9RJ0RTWXB9P6FV` Leland et al. 1994). Load generators that use Poisson arrivals are *still* an improvement over closed-loop, but Poisson understates burst intensity. The best honest generators support Pareto or MMPP processes; the ones that ship with Poisson understate real burst stress.

Think-time zeroing (`01KNZ4VB6JDWZF3NFVSD5ATJV8`, common-mistakes note) — running users back-to-back with no delay — is the other common mistake. It lets you hit higher aggregate RPS on the same VU count, so it looks more "efficient," but it destroys the session structure that caching, connection pooling, and autoscaling all depend on. The numbers are higher and the conclusions are wrong.

## 4. Metrics and statistical analysis — the solved problem nobody solved

The literature on how to measure latency honestly has been stable for ~15 years:

- **Averages lie; percentiles are closer; full distributions are honest** (`01KNZ4VB6JCJY0S4JYW2C3CHTR`).
- **Record samples in HdrHistogram**, not a moving average or a t-digest-only pipeline, if you care about tail correctness (`01KNZ4VB6JPYBYW64S7NNYS1CM`). O(1) record, fixed memory, exact percentiles up to a configured ceiling.
- **Coordinated omission is real and most tools still have it** (`01KNZ4VB6JB4Q5H3NPS72MZZ2A`). Gil Tene's "How NOT to Measure Latency" talk is ~11 years old; wrk2 (`01KNZ56MSNRKGMDGT9B745HVEB`) fixes it; wrk, JMeter, and most homegrown Python harnesses do not.
- **Percentiles do not compose.** You cannot average p99s across nodes or time windows and get something meaningful (`01KNZ4VB6JCD7A2BMXFN1AWGP4`). Merge the histograms, then take the percentile.
- **USE (`01KNZ4VB6JHP7W47HM7QREWW53`), RED (`01KNZ4VB6J6ED6F3YHN1SMDNQ5`), and Four Golden Signals (`01KNZ4VB6JXAZA2TBRCD5DERK9`)** are three overlapping checklists for what to collect. USE is resource-oriented (good for Gregg-style diagnosis), RED is request-oriented (good for service SLOs), Four Golden Signals is a superset with saturation added to RED. Pick one per service role.
- **Tail at Scale** (Dean &amp; Barroso, CACM 2013, `01KNZ4VB6JMSSE4E40PBE23S3M`) is the single most-cited paper for why tail latency dominates user experience at scale. It is the reason you never see "mean latency" SLOs at serious companies.

### Statistical validity — where honest tools fail

- **Warm-up and steady state** (`01KNZ4VB6JP254YSHY7N9PX4HQ`) must be detected, not assumed. JMH implements steady-state detection; most load-test tools report results that include warm-up samples and call it a day.
- **Run length matters.** Chen &amp; Revels 2016 (`01KNZ4VB6JEBFDN1QBC4680Y09`) show that microbenchmark result variance scales in non-obvious ways with run length; short runs give false precision.
- **Stabilizer** (Curtsinger &amp; Berger ASPLOS 2013, `01KNZ4VB6JZWDCTRVCP1R5V3GA`) randomises layout to turn environmental noise into *sound* noise; without Stabilizer or similar, ASPLOS-2013 showed that benchmark results can flip on compiler flag changes due to alignment luck.
- **Mytkowicz et al. 2009** (`01KNZ4VB6JF8CBPEK1YNFDTDAT`) — "Producing Wrong Data Without Doing Anything Obviously Wrong" — the paper every practitioner should read before claiming anything. Environment variable size, unused library linking, and directory names can flip your benchmark verdict.
- **Effect size is more important than p-value in regression testing** (`01KNZ67FDY7FH782T6V34Y2CFT` Cohen's d / Cliff's delta). Every CI perf tool reports p-values or fixed thresholds. None of them, at time of writing, report Cliff's delta. This is a trivial fix that would immediately improve signal-to-noise.

**Opinionated cut:** if you are building a perf-test tool today and your output is "mean latency: X, p95: Y, p99: Z," you have already failed. The right output is a distribution with confidence intervals, a non-parametric comparison to the baseline, and an effect-size bar alongside every p-value.

## 5. Tooling landscape — the scripting problem is solved

The state of the art in load generators is represented by the comparative matrix note `01KNZ5F8VABE5TGW976NMQA1VP`. Condensed judgment:

### Tier 1 — serious modern tools
- **k6** (`01KNZ56MPVSZD05KM395ZKAM5J`): best-in-class scripting model (JS runtime on Go core), proper open-loop scenarios, xk6 extensions for gRPC/browser/Kafka, thresholds-as-assertions, honest metrics. **Default recommendation for green-field.**
- **Gatling** (`01KNZ56MRW2B1XSH2X5K5AEJ33`): best DSL for complex simulations, honest open-loop by default, JVM ecosystem. Heavier than k6 but more expressive for complex journey modelling.
- **Fortio** (`01KNZ5F8TVW837C1YJKKXFH504`): Istio's QPS-targeted tool; unambiguously open-loop, good histogram reporting, gRPC native. Underrated.
- **wrk2** (`01KNZ56MSNRKGMDGT9B745HVEB`): the honest variant of wrk. If you want a minimal C HTTP benchmark that isn't lying, this is it.

### Tier 2 — honest-but-limited
- **Vegeta**: `unix-philosophy`, good histograms, constant-rate, HTTP-only. Fits pipelines; limited for stateful scenarios.
- **Locust**: Pythonic, easy to script, distributed mode. Default `FastHttpUser` is closed-loop and the docs don't shout this. Can be used correctly with explicit Poisson shapes but rarely is.
- **Artillery** / **NBomber**: language-native for JS / .NET; fine if those are your stacks, otherwise no compelling reason.

### Tier 3 — legacy incumbents
- **JMeter** (`01KNZ56MS27NFFMFBHZ0WPCV70`): still dominant in enterprise. XML test plans, GUI-led, thread-group (closed-loop by default), every known anti-pattern is a supported pattern. Use because compliance says so, not because it is good.
- **Tsung** / **Siege** / **hey** / **Bombardier** / **Drill** / **autocannon**: niche, legacy, or toy. `hey` and `autocannon` are fine for smoke-testing a dev endpoint; none are right for a real capacity test.

### SaaS platforms

BlazeMeter, Grafana Cloud k6, Azure Load Testing, AWS Distributed Load Testing, NeoLoad, LoadView, Loader.io. They solve execution-scale and geographic-distribution problems; they do not solve the workload-modelling problem. NeoLoad's MCP integration (`01KNZ5SMHEAYVDMJWJ9NAZBPCD`) is the most interesting recent development, not because it works well but because it signals that commercial vendors have decided LLM-assisted-test-authoring is a real product category.

### Profilers and continuous-profiling adjacent

Separate matrix at `01KNZ666W240KABAHAYZP98C3T`. The key recent shift is **continuous profiling in production** (`01KNZ666VRMB0N00T1E5GRPHT4`) — Pyroscope, Parca, Polar Signals, gProfiler — which is slowly replacing "run a profiler on a one-off repro" as the default mode of performance-regression investigation. This is important for test-generation tools because the production profile is the best ground truth for *which code paths actually matter*, which is exactly the information a test generator wants.

**Opinionated cut:** the load-generator market is saturated. The profiler market has consolidated around eBPF-based continuous profiling. The integration gap between them (microbenchmark regression → production profile delta correlation) is wide open and is #6 on my research-gap list below.

## 6. Performance test generation — the core focus

This is the section the vault was commissioned to answer. The ranked research-gaps synthesis lives at `01KNZ6QBKG64F2WEP3PNJK61JH`; this section reorganises the material along the five generation-source axes and takes a position on each.

### 6.1 From specifications (OpenAPI / GraphQL / gRPC / AsyncAPI)

**State of the art:** `openapi-generator-cli generate -g k6` (`01KNZ4TTX5V1TESBMRM80J38XA`), `postman-to-k6` (`01KNZ55NR8RQ3A2AM26Q7J92AG`), `har-to-k6` (`01KNZ55PD9MF8HE845RYWGYZ33`), Schemathesis (`01KNZ55NWZ1EH9FSVP5ZA6E4E7`), Dredd, ghz for gRPC (`01KNZ55P6BA1RE6Z5432ZYWG5W`).

**What works:** skeleton generation. Given a schema, the tool can produce a syntactically-valid k6 script that calls every endpoint once. This is a solid 80% time saving on the *boilerplate* of writing a load test.

**What fails (adversarially):**
- Schemas don't encode realistic *distributions*. Every request gets a fake-data blob; the payload-size distribution is uniform; there is no knowledge of which endpoints are called 10× more than others.
- Schemas don't encode *sequences*. A real user flow is `POST /login → GET /dashboard → POST /cart → POST /checkout` with resource-ID threading. Generated-from-schema scripts call endpoints in lexicographic order with fresh tokens every time.
- Schemas go out of date. Generated scripts rot.

**Toolmaker angle:** schema-to-script is a commodity. A *schema-plus-log-stats*-to-workload-model is not. A tool that takes an OpenAPI spec and a pre-aggregated access log and produces a properly-shaped workload (endpoint mix matching log frequency, payload sizes matching log distribution, sequences inferred from session IDs) would be genuinely new. Nobody ships it.

### 6.2 From production traffic (replay, shadow, mirror)

**State of the art:** GoReplay (`01KNZ5F4WQ8VSNJBFYJFDSX7NT`), Envoy tap filter (`01KNZ5F52X5746A9ASY0W6DKDS`), AWS VPC Traffic Mirroring (`01KNZ5F557YM1X8Q2ZBXZEBXRM`), Diffy (`01KNZ5F57EZAMVV3P5991NVHJ9`), ShadowReader (`01KNZ5ZPYD6VWX13H5G57D0TCH`), trace-driven load from Tempo/Jaeger (`01KNZ5F5C3YS1EYDCVFQ7TQS9H`).

**What works:** capture. Every layer of the stack now has a capture option — kernel (tcpreplay), sidecar (Envoy tap), cloud-native (VPC mirror), application (Jaeger).

**What fails:** the replay divergence problem (`01KNZ5F59R6M5ATD9X2YW87XAC`). Capture is the easy half; the hard half is:
1. **Time shifting** — replaying yesterday's capture against today's backend, when yesterday's tokens, resource IDs, and user state are all stale.
2. **PII scrubbing** — stripping user data without destroying the distribution of resource sizes.
3. **Amplification** — replaying at 10× rate without colliding on resource IDs (the canonical example: a capture that POSTs to create user `bob@example.com` cannot be replayed 100× without UNIQUE constraint failure).
4. **State management** — tokens expire, OAuth flows need replaying, idempotency keys need regeneration.
5. **Cross-system correlation** — a single user session often spans services; the capture needs to preserve and replay the cross-service ordering.

There is no open-source or commercial tool that solves the full pipeline. GoReplay solves (1) partially; Diffy solves (2) and (3) partially for diff testing but not for load; the rest is left to the user.

**Toolmaker angle:** an **Envoy-tap → canonical-IR → rate-controlled synthetic workload** pipeline is high-leverage. The capture format would be an intermediate representation (request template with parameterised fields, sequence graph, distribution metadata), and the generator would consume the IR to produce any target load tool's script. This is #3 on the ranked gap list.

### 6.3 From code analysis (static)

**State of the art:** almost nothing. Notes `01KNZ68K8H5MWD58PP5KVHD6QF` (static analysis to enumerate endpoints and hot paths) and `01KNZ68KB81AGB3MWRNS87EKXK` (pg_stat_statements as test seeds) document the primitives, but no integrated tool exists.

**Why it's hard:** static analysis can find N+1 query anti-patterns, identify regex use, flag unbounded loops — but connecting that to a *test* that exercises the path requires coverage-aware synthesis. The closest analogue is directed fuzzing (Driller, AFLGo, `01KNWE2QA8H1GKHCVNHYS5QW1F` worst-case input generation hub), which is closer to security-relevant perf pathology (ReDoS, hash floods) than to load-realism testing. The two lanes don't meet in any shipping tool.

**Toolmaker angle:** `pg_stat_statements` + `sqlcommenter` + a static analyser is a plausible low-hanging fruit — correlate slow production queries back to the HTTP endpoint that triggered them, then generate a load test that emphasises that endpoint. No tool does this end-to-end. Could be built in weeks rather than months.

### 6.4 From user-behaviour analytics (session mining)

**State of the art:** CBMG (`01KNZ5ZPPXW26VRNZ9BHKB0AYV`) is the 20-year-old canonical approach. Session mining from RUM logs (`01KNZ5ZPVXKFXJPF3MNWTYXMZ1`) is the empirical ingest. Åbo Akademi worst-case Markov traversals (`01KNZ5ZPSEK679QQYMHXF16WFF`) are the interesting variant. Workload drift (`01KNZ68KN59XANY9TX9WE0BYJH`) is the reason nothing stays current.

**What works:** session mining is a solved data-engineering problem — cluster users by behaviour, fit transition matrices. Off-the-shelf libraries (networkx, scikit-learn, pandas) support every step.

**What fails:** nobody has wired it to a load-test generator. The path from "I have RUM data" to "I have a k6 scenario that matches it" is artisanal, done by performance engineers in one-off notebooks. Menascé's textbook (`01KNZ6QBH0YZYKPZNYDCZD5P2B`) is the most complete treatment of the methodology and it is from 2001.

**Toolmaker angle:** the **session-mining → CBMG → load-test pipeline** is #1 on my ranked gap list for a reason. It is *conceptually* the most obvious thing in the world, technically straightforward, and ships as zero products. The only reason it hasn't happened, I think, is that the people who know CBMG don't ship tools and the people who ship load testers haven't read Menascé.

### 6.5 From LLMs (the frontier everyone is arguing about)

**State of the art (2023–2025):** DeepREST (`01KNZ5SMAD6NJG3EYE06C67A6S`), Fuzz4All (`01KNZ5SMCPQXVBGR1SZJCT57KV`), PerfGen (`01KNZ68KDQCP6HGTQEBGQW26VC`, arXiv 2412.04687), LlamaRestTest (`01KNZ6QBEK839FPJSX8KWET9TQ`), LLM-Based Fuzzing survey (`01KNZ6GWDH2RR758AACF6V2X3V`). Commercial: k6 Studio AI autocorrelation (`01KNZ5SMF1GAFA93P6D8TQFM4Z`), NeoLoad MCP (`01KNZ5SMHEAYVDMJWJ9NAZBPCD`). Capability-boundary hub: `01KNZ5SM642DR52PJ1CDNEZ101`.

**What LLMs actually do well:**
- Generate boilerplate k6/Gatling/Locust scripts from a textual description of "load test my /checkout endpoint at 500 RPS."
- Fill in fake-data templates for request bodies when given a schema.
- Pattern-match on existing test files to produce a variant that exercises a new endpoint.
- Repair drifted scripts when the API changes — the "self-healing" framing (`01KNZ5SMKSGB6GP479FNDRP1H3`).
- Summarise run output into "what got slower" narratives.

**What LLMs do badly:**
- **Pick a workload model.** Ask an LLM "what think-time distribution should I use for a B2B SaaS dashboard?" and you get a plausible-sounding, wrong number. There is no grounding data in the training corpus.
- **Understand open vs closed loop.** LLMs cheerfully emit `ramp-vus` scripts (closed-loop) when the correct answer is `constant-arrival-rate` (open-loop). The subtlety is not in the training data.
- **Reason about percentiles.** Output from LLM-generated analysis code routinely contains the p99-of-p99s bug.
- **Write oracles.** The LLM will happily emit `threshold: http_req_duration p(95) &lt; 500ms` without knowing whether 500ms is realistic for the endpoint in question.
- **Know when to fuzz vs. when to replay.** LLMs default to schema-driven generation because that is what most of their training examples look like.

**Honest framing:** LLMs are good at **reducing the writing effort** for tests a human has already specified, and bad at **specifying** the tests. The industry framing "the LLM writes the test" is marketing; the real framing is "the LLM drafts and the engineer edits." Tools that admit this (k6 Studio AI) do well; tools that hide it (NeoLoad MCP's marketing) set unrealistic expectations.

**Toolmaker angle:** LLM-assisted **workload-profile synthesis** — feed the LLM pre-aggregated log statistics and let it draft a workload YAML — is the right bet (#4 on the gap list). The LLM is doing a task it is good at (reading tabular data and producing structured output) on data it normally doesn't have (production statistics you provide). The oracle problem is still open, but the generation quality floor is much higher than "LLM writes script from prose."

### 6.6 Semi-automatic (recorders, service virtualization)

Recorders (`01KNZ6GW3GYN9ZDT3A9JTVJFEW`) are a mature commodity. Service virtualization (WireMock, Hoverfly, Mountebank; `01KNZ6GW6232G68HHVGR9ANYZM`) is the right answer to the environment-parity problem for upstream dependencies. Neither needs re-invention.

**Opinionated cut:** do not build another recorder. The space is saturated and the marginal user value is near zero.

## 7. Performance testing in CI/CD

### The gating problem

The CI/CD performance-gating lane (cluster D) covers regression detection, flakiness mitigation, and the oracle problem.

**Canonical reading order:**
1. Daly et al. 2020 ICPE (`01KNZ67FBPEC378X6KZ79305T0`) — MongoDB's change-point detection system. The paper that made CPD credible for perf regression. E-divisive as the statistical core.
2. Hunter 2023 (`01KNZ67FCARB8N2V5KPN8TY1PG`) — the follow-up tool paper from the same team.
3. Mytkowicz 2009 (`01KNZ6FPQ32R61BEN1K1WNZGPX`) — why your benchmark result is probably noise.
4. Stabilizer 2013 (`01KNZ6FPS6S89303Q1F11G6M1A`) — the honest statistical alternative (randomise to make noise sound).
5. Chen &amp; Revels 2016 (`01KNZ4VB6JEBFDN1QBC4680Y09`) — robust benchmarking of real code.
6. Barr et al. 2015 (`01KNZ72G2VNNP6JHWQAK0HJTXM`) — the oracle-problem survey.

### The four orthogonal answers to CI noise

The literature gives four non-overlapping fixes for CI benchmark noise. Most teams pick one without knowing the others exist.

1. **Randomise the environment.** Stabilizer. Turns environmental bias into statistical noise; then classical inference works. Academic, not deployed in industry.
2. **Use robust non-parametric statistics.** Mann-Whitney U (`01KNZ67FDMCEA0MKZ8GZ841NDT`), Cliff's delta (`01KNZ67FDY7FH782T6V34Y2CFT`), bootstrap CIs. Adopted by criterion.rs, partially by a few CI tools. **The most practical option for most teams.**
3. **Change the metric from wall-clock to something deterministic.** CodSpeed (`01KNZ6T74XZWE3RYQ86DZ2WREJ`) uses Valgrind to count instructions; bencher.dev (`01KNZ6T759YNNAFPCMPAGSTCYV`) exposes multiple backends. Instruction counts are stable but can drift from wall-clock under microarchitectural changes.
4. **Accept the noise and use change-point detection.** Daly's approach. Treat the time series as a noisy stochastic process; detect step changes rather than single-run regressions. Adopted by MongoDB, Mozilla Perfherder (`01KNZ6T75SFGWWGZPNF1AXR09K`), Chrome Pinpoint (`01KNZ6T765RV5SX44WKJKCEPVJ`).

Each approach has a failure mode. Randomisation slows execution and is infeasible at CI cadence. Non-parametric stats still need *n* samples per commit and CI budget is limited. Instruction counting diverges from user-observed latency. Change-point detection needs history, so it fails for new benchmarks and for long-gap releases.

**Opinionated cut:** a serious CI perf gate in 2026 should combine (2) and (4) — non-parametric per-commit with change-point across history — and should report effect size, not p-values. Every tool on the market does only one of the four, and the aggregate alert quality is visibly bad (see Japke et al. 2025, `01KNZ6782C4J9WBCRJ12NHYZN3X`, for empirical pain).

### Canary analysis and progressive delivery

Kayenta (`01KNZ6T721S1YTYHGZE1AS1Y43`), Argo Rollouts / Flagger. These are *production* perf gates — the "canary as oracle" pattern: deploy to a small subset, compare metrics to baseline, promote or roll back. The statistical engine in Kayenta is Mann-Whitney, which is the right choice, but the effect-size blindness applies here too: Kayenta will fail a deploy on a statistically significant 0.3% latency increase that no user will notice.

**Complementary to pre-merge CI perf gates.** Do both. Pre-merge catches the "algorithm got worse" regressions; canary catches the "interaction with real traffic distribution" regressions. Neither replaces the other.

### The oracle problem, applied to performance

Barr et al. 2015 (`01KNZ72G2VNNP6JHWQAK0HJTXM`) cataloguesfour oracle kinds: specification-based, derived, implicit, and none. Applied to perf:

- **Specification-based oracles = SLOs** (`01KNZ4VB6JQZHJVB2EQK6HVXE0`). The cleanest case — "must meet p99 &lt; X at load Y."
- **Derived oracles = historical baselines.** What CPD and microbenchmark regression do.
- **Implicit oracles = doesn't crash, doesn't error, doesn't OOM.** Catches catastrophic regressions, misses subtle ones.
- **No oracle = reports numbers, human decides.** What most ad-hoc perf testing actually is.

**Metamorphic relations** (Segura et al. 2018, `01KNZ72G5955YGB9B2W61QD2Z4`) are the promising underused alternative: express invariants like "doubling input size should at most double latency," then mechanically generate pairs of inputs and check the relation. Performance metamorphic testing is *severely* under-implemented — no mainstream tool, zero commercial products, one proof-of-concept paper. This is a real gap.

## 8. Research frontier — open problems

The research gaps note at `01KNZ6QBKG64F2WEP3PNJK61JH` ranks concrete tooling targets. Here I focus on the *conceptual* open problems that any tool-builder should have a position on.

### 8.1 The workload-drift problem

Production traffic evolves continuously; test suites are static. Over months, the correlation between "what the test measures" and "what the user experiences" decays silently. There is no quantitative definition of drift, no standard detector, no update protocol. Japke et al. 2025 (`01KNZ6782C4J9WBCRJ12NHYZN3X`) gesture at the problem; nobody solves it.

**Claim:** any production-grade test-generation tool needs a drift telemetry component or it becomes wrong within a year of adoption.

### 8.2 The environment-parity problem

"Works on my benchmark rig" is the perf equivalent of "works on my machine." Google has fleet-wide profiling; most teams have a shared load-test VM with a noisy neighbour. Mytkowicz-style measurement bias is the norm, not the exception.

**Claim:** this is not a tool problem, it is an organisational problem. No amount of clever statistics rescues results taken on an inadequate rig. A plausible *tool* intervention is automated environment fingerprinting — refuse to report results if the runner differs too much from a known-good profile.

### 8.3 The oracle problem, unsolved part

SLOs work when they exist. Most teams don't have them. Metamorphic perf relations work in principle, are unimplemented in practice. Change-point detection works historically, fails for cold starts. Differential / A-B comparison works cross-version, fails when the "baseline" is itself drifting.

**Claim:** the tool that ships a *composable oracle layer* — let users plug in SLO thresholds, metamorphic relations, CPD history, and differential comparison, and get a unified verdict — captures the perf-gating market. #2 on my ranked gap list.

### 8.4 The microbenchmark-to-application-benchmark bridge

Japke et al. (`01KNZ6782CH21JYMYXTQT9D8W5B`) point out that microbenchmarks and application benchmarks detect different regressions and that neither is strictly better. The open problem: can you predict which microbenchmark regressions will translate to application regressions?

**Claim:** this is the *most empirically tractable* open problem in the vault. With the Besbes et al. 2025 Perfherder dataset (`01KNZ6782BP23F4QQHXDNBVEZX1`), you can train a classifier on historical microbench/app-bench pairs and measure translation accuracy. Somebody should publish this paper.

### 8.5 LLMs and the specification gap

LLMs generate tests; the *specification* of what the test should check is left to humans. The tool that closes that gap — LLM drafts the specification, human approves, LLM generates the test to match — is the right product shape, and nobody has built it.

**Claim:** this is the LLM-for-perf-testing product that actually matters, and it is not what any of the current LLM-perf-test papers are about.

## 9. Ranked product gaps (toolmaker's shortlist)

This section compresses §6.1–8.5 into a build-vs-don't-build ranked list. Details and feasibility estimates are in `01KNZ6QBKG64F2WEP3PNJK61JH`; the short version:

1. **Session-mining → CBMG → load-test pipeline.** High leverage, high feasibility, moderate adoption. 4–8 engineer-weeks for v1. Nobody ships it.
2. **Composable statistical oracle library** (SLO + metamorphic + CPD + A/B, unified verdict). High leverage, high feasibility, high adoption. Medium commitment.
3. **Envoy-tap / GoReplay → canonical-IR → rate-controlled synthetic workload.** Medium leverage (solves half the replay-divergence problem), medium feasibility, medium adoption. Requires opinionated IR design.
4. **LLM-assisted workload-profile synthesis from log statistics.** Medium leverage, high feasibility, high adoption. The LLM product that actually works for perf testing.
5. **Session-level latency-reward fuzzing (RESTler + latency oracle).** High research novelty, lower commercial adoption. Build as research tool first.

Honourable mentions: workload drift detector; AsyncAPI → Kafka load generator; pg_stat_statements → HTTP endpoint attribution via sqlcommenter; Tempo/Jaeger → k6 scenario bridge; LLM-drafted SLO documents from PRDs.

**Do not build:** another recorder, another load-generator-from-OpenAPI wrapper, an LLM that writes k6 scripts from prose. These spaces are saturated or solve the wrong problem.

## 10. What I would tell a product lead starting tomorrow

- **The scripting problem is solved.** Do not build a better k6.
- **The measurement problem has been solved for 15 years but the market still gets it wrong.** Ship HdrHistogram + open-loop-by-default + effect-size-aware CI gating and you will outcompete everyone on honest numbers.
- **The workload problem is the whole ball game.** The team that builds the first honest session-mining-to-workload pipeline gets the category.
- **The oracle problem is the second ball game.** Composable oracles or metamorphic relations win the regression-gating category.
- **LLMs are a feature, not a product.** LLM-drafts-script is a pleasant add-on to a tool that already does workload mining well. LLM-writes-the-test is a demo, not a business.
- **Buy time with a good demo on one real customer's production logs.** The whole category will lose credibility to vaporware pitches; the team that demos on real log data wins hearts.
